\begin{center}
{\bfseries Abstract}
\end{center}

Retrieval practice is well established as a valuable mechanism for
enhancing information retention and improving academic performance in
students. (Roediger et al., 2006) However, in the fast-paced learning
environment of Singapore primary schools, teachers have to cover a
packed syllabus, and revision practices also take time to craft, making
it difficult for teachers to facilitate structured retrieval practices
of taught topics. The platform outlined in this report aims to address
this problem, and provide a software that enables teachers to easily
generate, schedule and monitor short revision quizzes for students. The
platform will also make use of gamification features to engage and
retain students, allowing for consistent participation in retrieval
practice and improving students' learning outcomes.

\hypertarget{chapter-1-introduction}{%
\chapter{Chapter 1 --- Introduction}\label{chapter-1-introduction}}

Retrieval practice has been shown to be a highly effective strategy for
strengthening long-term information retention (Roediger et al., 2006). Compared to passive
review of study material, actively recalling information from memory
improves retention of that information. However, despite the strong
evidence supporting retrieval practice, systematic implementation in Singapore
primary school classrooms is limited. A major barrier is the time and effort
required for teachers to facilitate
effective retrieval practice. It remains challenging to create
high-quality quizzes that are well-aligned with their curriculum and
learning objectives, and class time is generally used to cover an
already packed syllabus, leaving little time for additional activities.

This project aims to address this barrier by developing a system that
facilitates the easy creation, management and assignment of revision
quizzes. The system will be supplemented by an AI-powered quiz
generation system that can automatically create quizzes using
provided instructions and content. The end goal is to provide
teachers with an efficient tool for creating and assigning effective
retrieval practice materials, ultimately enhancing student learning
outcomes.

The project consists of two main user-facing components:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  A teacher-facing web dashboard where teachers can create and manage
  their quizzes, schedule quiz assignments, and view student performance
  analytics. A dedicated AI quiz generation interface is included,
  assisting teachers in creating quizzes by taking in instructions and
  content, then generating draft quizzes aligned to provided
  specifications. The interface will allow easy review and editing of
  AI-generated quizzes before finalisation.
\item
  A mobile application that allows students to access and complete their
  assigned quizzes on their phones. The mobile application is meant to
  provide a simple, clean and user-friendly interface for students to
  engage with their quizzes and receive immediate feedback on their
  performance. The app will also include features such as progress
  tracking and gamification elements to motivate students to regularly
  engage in retrieval practice.
\end{enumerate}

This platform is currently named Ember, signifying the steady flame of learning that
the platform aims to ignite in students through consistent retrieval practice.
From preliminary user studies and feedback, this system is generally well received by
teachers, who find the overall value of the platform to be high, and the retrieval practice
and AI generation features to be particularly useful.

\hypertarget{chapter-2-literature-review}{%
\chapter{Chapter 2 --- Literature Review}\label{chapter-2-literature-review}}

\hypertarget{pedagogical-rationale}{%
\section{2.1 Pedagogical Rationale}\label{pedagogical-rationale}}

\hypertarget{core-idea-practice-testing-retrieval}{%
\subsection{2.1.1 Core idea: Practice Testing
(Retrieval)}\label{core-idea-practice-testing-retrieval}}

This platform is built around retrieval practice, which is the act of
using low or no-stakes activities that require students to retrieve
target information from memory. In many laboratory and
classroom-adjacent studies, attempting retrieval of content, as opposed
to other revision methods, produces significantly better long-term
retention of study material.

A simple demonstration comes from Roediger \& Karpicke (2006). Learners
were shown short expository sciences passages, after which they were
split into two groups. Group A restudied the passage for 7 minutes,
while Group B completed a 7 minute free-recall test - essentially
writing everything they could remember. On the final free-recall tests
performed at 2 days and 1 week from the initial study, the practice-test
group outperformed the restudy group in recall accuracy, with 68\% vs
54\% for 2 days, and 56\% vs 42\% for 1 week.

\hypertarget{volume-of-tests}{%
\subsection{2.1.2 Volume of Tests}\label{volume-of-tests}}

Another crucial factor that impacts information retention is the volume
of tests. Roediger \& Karpicke (2008) compared practice schedules across
four periods: SSSS (restudy only), SSST (mixed), and STTT (test-heavy).
After 1 week, they tracked student performance based on the number of
tests given. STTT forgot least (\textasciitilde10\%) and SSSS most
(\textasciitilde52\%), indicating that an increase in the number of
tests had a positive effect on the information retained.

\hypertarget{optimal-spacing-of-tests}{%
\subsection{2.1.3 Optimal Spacing of
Tests}\label{optimal-spacing-of-tests}}

Further studies have also indicated that the interval between these
practice tests are also significant. In a case of paired foreign-word
learning, Pyc \& Rawson (2012) manipulated the schedules of practice
tests for different learners. Their findings indicate that when practice
tests were spaced with longer gaps to make recall of content more
difficult, students scored higher on the later test as compared to
students that had practice tests with much shorter gaps.

\hypertarget{implementation-of-practice-testing-in-singapore}{%
\subsection{2.1.4 Implementation of Practice Testing in
Singapore}\label{implementation-of-practice-testing-in-singapore}}

Unfortunately, implementing practice testing in the Singaporean
classroom is not a trivial task. The syllabus is packed, and teachers
move from topic to topic quickly. (Ratnam-Lim \& Tan, 2015). That makes
it unrealistic for teachers to consistently hand-craft new recall items
and orchestrate revisit points for every topic.

\hypertarget{use-of-quizzes-and-games-in-education}{%
\section{2.2 Use of Quizzes and Games in
Education}\label{use-of-quizzes-and-games-in-education}}

\hypertarget{definition-of-gamification}{%
\subsection{2.2.1 Definition of
Gamification}\label{definition-of-gamification}}

Gamification refers to the use of selected game elements such as points,
badges, leaderboards, avatars, progress bars, streaks, in non-game
contexts (Deterding et al., 2011).

\hypertarget{use-of-gamification-in-education}{%
\subsection{2.2.2 Use of Gamification in
Education}\label{use-of-gamification-in-education}}

In the field of education, substantial evidence indicates that
gamification of learning activities can improve cognitive outcomes and
motivation in students, especially when gamification mechanics are
coupled with explicit learning goals and timely, informational feedback.
In a meta-analysis done by Sailer \& Homner (2020) and a review done by
Clark, Tanner and Killingsworth (2016), classes that made use of
gamification tools in learning did noticeably better on tasks and were
more motivated to learn relative to nongame conditions.

Similarly, a systematic study of Kahoot!, an online quiz based platform
with gamification elements, concluded that when used as brief and
frequent activities, it improves learning performance, student attitudes
and can reduce anxiety (Wang \& Tahir, 2020).

\hypertarget{findings-on-specific-gamification-elements}{%
\subsection{2.2.3 Findings on Specific Gamification
Elements}\label{findings-on-specific-gamification-elements}}

More specifically, studies have shown that gamification elements such as
points, rewards, leaderboards and achievement streaks (Olszewski, 2025) can improve
student performance in learning when implemented as informational
progress cues rather than as controlling incentives or pure competition,
(Mekler et al., 2017). Other classroom studies report neutral effects of
these elements on achievement but positive attitudes toward continued
use, indicating that gamification elements should be used as engagement
scaffolds rather than learning mechanisms (Balci et al., 2022).

Another form of gamification that has found success is avatar
identification and customisation. Lab studies have shown that users who
were given the option to personalise an avatar with cosmetic items
(outfits, accessories) were shown to have more intrinsic motivation to
complete tasks (Birk et al., 2016). Moreover, studies of game design
have found that customisation and self-expression features support key
needs from Self-Determination Theory, especially autonomy and
relatedness, which improves motivation and replay (Turkay \& Adinolf,
2015). Sinha (2025) also emphasised the importance of positive reinforcement
in promoting consistent participation and timely submissions. In particular,
progressive reward systems, such as points and badges, are effective in
rewarding effort and persistence.

\hypertarget{implementation-of-gamification-elements}{%
\subsection{2.2.4 Implementation of Gamification
Elements}\label{implementation-of-gamification-elements}}

Overall, studies on educational gamification link improved student
performance to increased participation and persistence. Importantly,
gamification is ancillary to learning. Its purpose is to complement the
practice-testing mechanisms by increasing frequency and regularity of
practice to produce the positive effects of practice testing as outlined
in Section 2.1. Accordingly, the gamification features for the proposed
platform will be designed to encourage sustained use, and these will be
outlined in Chapter 8.

\hypertarget{use-of-large-language-models-llms-to-generate-practice-testing-content}{%
\section{2.3 Use of Large Language Models (LLMs) to Generate
Practice-Testing
Content}\label{use-of-large-language-models-llms-to-generate-practice-testing-content}}

\hypertarget{llms-as-quiz-item-drafters}{%
\subsection{2.3.1 LLMs as Quiz Item
Drafters}\label{llms-as-quiz-item-drafters}}

Recent empirical evidence indicates that LLMs such as GPT-3.5 and 4 can
draft multiple-choice and short-answer items that are comparable in
clarity and key validity to instructor-written items while taking
substantially less time to author. In a PLOS ONE study, independent
raters judged ChatGPT-generated MCQs for graduate medical exams as
indistinguishable in overall quality from professor-written items
(Cheung, 2023). Authors also documented a large authoring-time advantage
when making use of ChatGPT. Further evaluations in the fields of
medicine revealed similar results. After vetting for alignment and
distractors, ChatGPT/GPT-4-generated items match or approach human items
in appropriateness, clarity/specificity, suitability, and psychometric
properties (Kiyak et al., 2024) (Bhandari et al., 2024). In the field of
data science, LLM-generated retrieval practice has also shown to be
effective at improving learning outcomes (An, 2025).

Outside of higher education, a study that generated and field-tested
K-12 (primary and secondary school) items found that LLM-generated
questions were comparable on accuracy and validity when compared to
human-generated baselines (Zelikman et al., 2023).

\hypertarget{common-weaknesses-and-practical-mitigations}{%
\subsection{2.3.2 Common Weaknesses and Practical
Mitigations}\label{common-weaknesses-and-practical-mitigations}}

Across multiple studies, evaluations and reviews, several recurring
problems appear when using LLM to draft assessment items. Firstly, when
an LLM isn't given precise constraints, wording might drift away from the
intended syllabus, difficulty level and target idea (Kiyak et al., 2024)
(Al Shuraiqi et al., 2024) (Law et al., 2025). Secondly, generated items might be
ambiguous, having more than one interpretation, or having multiple
reasonable answers (Bouamor et al., 2023) (Florian, 2025). Finally,
models can sometimes introduce factual slips, also known as
hallucinations. This could be the most harmful of all, as the model
might generate items that look reasonable, and would require discerning
eyes to fix (An et al., 2025) (Masters, 2023).

To mitigate the above pitfalls, Kıyak et al.~(2024) finds that general
prompt engineering strategies as documented by OpenAI were effective in
improving item output. In his prompts for generation, requests were
structured with the syllabus topic, difficulty level, cognitive target,
and the expected answer form.

Elzayyat et al.~(2025) finds that expert review on generated items
remains critical to ensure clarity and alignment with curricular goals.
Artsi et al.~(2024) similarly emphasises that a teacher or SME should
review the generated items to catch ambiguity and hallucinations. These
conclusions point to the importance of a ``human-gate'' to audit the
generated content before release.

As such, the proposed platform plans to implement the AI quiz generation
features with structured prompts and a teacher-in-the-loop workflow that
encodes the above mentioned safeguards by design.

\hypertarget{actionable-analytics-to-guide-formative-decisions}{%
\section{2.4 Actionable Analytics to Guide Formative
Decisions}\label{actionable-analytics-to-guide-formative-decisions}}

Teachers have been shown to make better instructional decisions when
they have timely and interpretable evidence about their students'
understanding and struggles. Classic formative assessment research has
shown that using assessment evidence to guide teaching can raise overall
student achievement, with particularly strong effectiveness for
lower-achieving students (Black \& Wiliam, 1998). A study on
instructor-facing analytics also reported that learning analytics was
regarded by many teachers to be an important tool to support them in
revising syllabus content or providing good intervention to students
(Wise \& Jung, 2019). In an exam-oriented system like Singapore where
ongoing formative use is still challenging in practice (Ratnam-Lim \& Tan,
2015), concise analytics that surface where students are lacking can be
especially valuable for teachers.

\hypertarget{commonly-used-educational-platforms-with-similar-goals}{%
\section{2.5 Commonly Used Educational Platforms with Similar
Goals}\label{commonly-used-educational-platforms-with-similar-goals}}

Digital platforms are widely encouraged in Singapore classrooms
(Ministry of Education Singapore, 2024), but they tend to serve
complementary, distinct use cases rather than the very specific one
targeted here (brief, phone-first retrieval practice with teacher-gated
AI authoring and concise, topic-level analytics).

\hypertarget{kahoot-teacher-paced-live-quiz-games-optional-homework-mode.}{%
\subsection{2.5.1 Kahoot! (teacher-paced, live quiz games, optional
homework
mode).}\label{kahoot-teacher-paced-live-quiz-games-optional-homework-mode.}}

Synthesis work on classroom use of Kahoot! consistently reports
improvements in test performance, classroom dynamics, and
student/teacher attitudes, especially when run as short, in-class
episodes with immediate feedback  (Wang \& Tahir, 2020). However, Kahoot!'s
core design is optimised for synchronous, whole-class play. While it has
an assignment mode, the predominant use pattern is still live classroom
sessions rather than routine quizzing meant to target retrieval
practice.

\hypertarget{quizlet-learner-driven-flashcards-and-practice-modes-including-spaced-study.}{%
\subsection{2.5.2 Quizlet (learner-driven flashcards and practice modes,
including spaced
study).}\label{quizlet-learner-driven-flashcards-and-practice-modes-including-spaced-study.}}

Empirical studies and recent syntheses relating to Quizlet generally
find small-to-moderate gains in vocabulary/term learning and retention,
with positive learner attitudes, particularly when practice is regular
and spaced. (Özdemir, 2024) Design-wise, however, Quizlet is primarily
oriented toward self-initiated study sets. It lacks in teacher-assigned,
syllabus-specific workflows and class-level analytics

\hypertarget{koobits}{%
\subsection{2.5.3 KooBits}\label{koobits}}

Among current tools, KooBits has the greatest overlap with the present
project's audience and curriculum. KooBits emphasises alignment to the
Singapore MOE syllabus (Mathematics, Science, English), daily short
practice with auto-marked questions, and broad school-level adoption.
However, two gaps remain relative to the use case targeted.

Mobile, on-the-go practice. Student access is mostly done via web
browsers on computers, whereas the proposed system is phone-first by
design, with short quizzes that launch with a single tap and are
intended for interstitial moments such as commutes.

KooBits intentionally bundles many capabilities (curriculum content,
games, animations, challenges, rewards, school/parent portals). The
breadth is valuable, but for younger learners it could potentially raise
navigational and decision overhead (Firat \& Kuzu, 2011) which may lead
to negative performance gains. In contrast, the proposed application is
deliberately single-purpose. Quizzes are the main surface, and they are
low-friction to start, reducing extraneous steps and keeping attention
on practice testing.

Taken together, existing platforms excel at in-class engagement
(Kahoot!), general assessment delivery, self-paced term learning with
spacing (Quizlet), and they offer ecosystem breadth specific to the
Singapore syllabus (KooBits). Few, however, are designed specifically
for brief, phone-optimised quizzes intended for daily use. The
combination of routine, low-friction practice testing, with rapid
content generation and actionable analytics, is precisely the gap the
proposed platform is designed to fill. It operationalises the frequency
and spacing conditions under which retrieval practice yields the
strongest long-term retention (Section 2.1), while minimising planning
and marking overhead for teachers.

\hypertarget{system-overview-and-report-structure}{%
\section{3.1 System Overview and Report
Structure}\label{system-overview-and-report-structure}}

The system is designed around the operationalisation of
retrieval-practice principles in a way that is feasible for Singapore
primary school teachers to implement. This requires an efficient,
user-friendly platform for teachers to author, assign and monitor
quizzes, as well as a simple, easy-to-navigate mobile application for
students to access and complete their quizzes. The platform is
intentionally scoped to be a tool that supports teachers and enhances
their existing teaching practices, rather than a standalone learning
system. It should empower teachers to implement retrieval practice more
effectively and efficiently.

The platform's scope consists of a teacher-facing web dashboard, a
student-facing mobile application, and a backend separated into multiple
domain-based microservices for identity, quiz lifecycle,
class/scheduling, AI generation, and gamification. A full, comprehensive
requirement specification is provided in Appendix A.

In the following chapters of the report, each domain will be broken down
and explained. These will include design choices, implementation
methods, and the main tradeoffs or limitations that follow from them.

\hypertarget{functional-requirements}{%
\section{3.2 Functional Requirements}\label{functional-requirements}}

For the functional requirements, the platform is designed around two core user
journeys. This includes the overall teacher journey for preparing and assigning
retrieval practice, and the student journey for attempting that retrieval practice
with the least amount of friction possible.

For teachers, the main journey is:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Create an account and set up a profile
\item
  Sign in securely
\item
  Create a class and generate student accounts with credentials
\item
  Create or manage practice quizzes using different quiz types
\item
  Schedule retrieval practice quizzes for classes with specific rules such as
  attempt windows, attempt limits and score contribution.
\item
  Review student performance afterwards through class, student and quiz based analytics.
\end{enumerate}

For students, the main journey is intentionally designed to be simple, to reduce
the complexity of the overall experience and encourage regular use.

The main student journey is:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Sign in with their credentials
\item
  See the retrieval practice quizzes that are currently available for them
  to attempt, assigned by their teachers.
\item
  Start or resume their attempts in quiz interfaces optimised for a mobile experience.
\item
  Submit answers after completing the quiz.
\item
  Immediately review results and progress after attempt submission.
\end{enumerate}

Separately, as supporting infrastructure for the above journeys, there are two
key sets of functional requirements that are not part of any key user journey
but are still critical to the overall value of the platform.

The first is a teacher-in-the-loop AI workflow, as defined in Section 2.3.
Teachers should be able to provide instructions and source material, and the
system should use LLMs to generate draft quizzes, formatted in supported quiz
types by the platform. Teachers should then be able to review and edit the generated
quizzes, before assignment to students. This workflow is designed to make the
most tedious part of the key teacher journey (quiz authoring) more efficient,
while still ensuring that teachers have full control over the content and quality
of the quizzes that they assign to their students.

The second is a set of gamification features that are designed to encourage the
key student journey of regular retrieval practice. The functional requirements
for these features include scoring, leaderboards, streaks, rewards and customisable
avatars and profiles.

\hypertarget{non-functional-requirements}{%
\section{3.3 Non-Functional
Requirements}\label{non-functional-requirements}}

The core non-functional requirements help to ensure that the system executes
the functional requirements in a reliable, consistent and secure manner.

These requirements fall under four main categories: integrity,
reliability, security, and maintainability.

Integrity requirements focus on system consistency and correctness.
Core records should remain accurate throughout all operations,
and derived or linked records should stay
aligned with their source of truth.

Reliability requirements focus on resilience under stress and
distributed conditions The system should be able to tolerate retries,
partial outages, and delayed event delivery without corrupting state.

Security requirements focus on data protection and privacy.
Routes should be secured with access tokens issued by a central
authority. Internal routes between services must be secured with
secrets. Upload constraints and AI generation limits should also be used
to mitigate abuse and prevent exhaustion of available resources.

Maintainability requirements focus on ease of development and upkeep.
The system should be designed to minimise cross-domain regression by
maintaining a modular and decoupled architecture.  Each service should
have clear boundaries and a well defined API for interactions.

\hypertarget{high-level-architecture}{%
\section{3.4 High-Level Architecture}\label{high-level-architecture}}

The platform adopts a microservice architecture with explicit service
boundaries. Instead of concentrating all
business logic in a single backend, each domain is owned by a dedicated
service with specific responsibilities. This approach improves
maintainability, simplifies fault isolation, and reduces regression risk
during development.

\hypertarget{domain-owned-backend-services}{%
\subsection{3.4.2 Domain-Owned Backend
Services}\label{domain-owned-backend-services}}

The core backend services of the platform is as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{User Service}: authentication, token issuance, teacher/student
  account management, and authorisation workflows.
\item
  \textbf{Quiz Service}: quiz authoring , version control, attempt
  management and grading logic.
\item
  \textbf{Class Service}: class/scheduling management and analytics
  projections.
\item
  \textbf{AI Service}: asynchronous quiz generation workflows.
\item
  \textbf{Game Service}: leaderboard projections, streaks, rewards,
  badges, inventory/loadout management, avatar rendering, and reward
  related notifications.
\end{enumerate}

The backend is organised so that each service owns its data and has its
own rules. Cross-service interaction happens only through clearly
defined APIs and event flows, and no services are allowed writes into
the persistence of other services. This boundary ensures that the system
stays modular and decoupled, with services being able to evolve independently
based on their needs.

\hypertarget{infrastructure-layer}{%
\subsection{3.4.3 Infrastructure Layer}\label{infrastructure-layer}}

Each service runs in an isolated container and persists data in its own
MongoDB database. Event transport is handled through Kafka. Redis is
used where time-sensitive events are required, such as enforcing time
limits placed on quiz attempts.

An Nginx gateway is also used to provide stable ingress and service
routing.

\hypertarget{service-communication-and-deployment}{%
\section{3.5 Service Communication and
Deployment}\label{service-communication-and-deployment}}

\hypertarget{synchronous-api-calls}{%
\subsection{3.5.1 Synchronous API Calls}\label{synchronous-api-calls}}

Synchronous HTTP calls are used when immediate responses are required
within a request lifecycle. Typical examples include frontend-to-service
requests and service-to-service checks that require real-time
validation, such as identity checks for access control. This
communication mode is used where immediate responses are required.

\hypertarget{asynchronous-event-flows}{%
\subsection{3.5.2 Asynchronous Event
Flows}\label{asynchronous-event-flows}}

Asynchronous event messaging is used for cross-domain updates that do
not need to complete in the initiating HTTP call. Publishing services do
not need to be aware of downstream consumers, and they can simply publish
events when their own state changes. It allows for decoupling of services
while still supporting eventual consistency across the system.

This design was preferred over a tightly coupled synchronous API call
approach, as request paths would become longer, more fragile, and more
difficult to maintain as the number of cross-service interactions grows.
It also allows for a more responsive user experience, as
user-facing actions can return immediately without needing downstream
processing to complete.

Kafka was chosen to be used as the backbone for event transport due to
its high throughput, scalability, and durability. It is also well suited
for a system where the same event might need to be consumed by multiple
services, as Kafka's pub/sub model allows for multiple independent
consumers to subscribe to the same event stream without interfering with
each other.

The system also uses a durable outbox pattern. Instead of emitting events
directly to Kafka, events are first written
to a durable outbox table in the emitter's database. Events in the outbox
are only marked as emitted after they have been successfully sent to Kafka,
and a worker ensures that the order of events is preserved.

Consumers of the events are also designed to be idempotent.
All events are keyed with an id, and consumers discard duplicates
before applying any updates.

\hypertarget{deployment-and-runtime-topology}{%
\subsection{3.5.3 Deployment and Runtime
Topology}\label{deployment-and-runtime-topology}}

The platform runs in a containerised topology, and each service is
independently deployable. This supports incremental scaling and reduces
the blast radius of failures within one service, as functionalities are
mostly isolated within containers.

Services are also designed to be stateless, with state persisted in
their own databases or through event flows. This allows for horizontal
scaling and simplifies deployment and maintenance.

Wherever appropriate, background processing is also separated from
standard request-response paths (for example, event
publishing/consumption flows are separated to a separate worker
service). This prevents other endpoints of a service from being blocked
even as other longer-running internal tasks are being executed.

Overall, the platform's architecture is designed with a focus on
clarity, extensibility, and reliability for real classroom usage.


\hypertarget{chapter-4-user-service-authentication-and-authorisation}{%
\chapter{Chapter 4 --- User Service, Authentication, and
Authorisation}\label{chapter-4-user-service-authentication-and-authorisation}}

The User Service serves as a centralised service for all functionalities
related to identity, credential management, authentication and
authorisation. All other services in the platform rely on the User
Service as the sole authority for role management and access control.

This design helps to ensure that authentication and role-based access
control are consistent throughout the entire application. It also allows
downstream services to focus solely on functionalities in their own
domain, while simply calling on User Service endpoints for account state
and access control logic.

\hypertarget{teacher-accounts-and-verification}{%

For sign-up:

The email verification step provides a critical recovery path for teachers,

\hypertarget{authentication-verification-and-recovery-flows}{%
\section{4.2 Student Account Journeys}\label{authentication-verification-and-recovery-flows}}

The student journeys are designed with the assumption that primary school
students might not yet have sufficient technical knowledge to manage their own accounts.
Therefore, most account management workflows are routed through the teacher, allowing
for a more guided and controlled experience for students.

\hypertarget{student-accounts-and-teacher-ownership}{%
\subsection{4.2.1 Teacher Guided Account Access
}\label{student-accounts-and-teacher-ownership}}

Student Account Access Flow:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Teacher creates student accounts under their class and generates credentials for them.
  \item Teacher shares credentials with students.
  \item Students use the credentials to sign in for the first time.
  \item Students are prompted to change their password after first sign in,
        and they can then access the platform in the future with their new credentials.
\end{enumerate}

Each student account is therefore ``owned'' by a teacher, allowing future student account management flows
to go through the owning teacher rather than the students.

\hypertarget{student-sign-in}{%
\subsection{4.2.2 Teacher-Guided
Recovery}\label{student-sign-in}}

Account Recovery Flow:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Student tells their teacher (that owns their account) that they have forgotten their password or are having trouble signing in.
  \item The teacher can trigger a password reset for the student from the dashboard, which will generate a temporary password for the teacher.
  \item The teacher shares the temporary password with the student.
  \item The student can then use the temporary password to sign in.
\end{enumerate}

To support this workflow securely, the system restricts password resets of student accounts to
only the owning teacher, and the temporary password generated is only valid for the first
sign in, after which the student is required to change it again to a new password.

\hypertarget{centralised-authorisation-and-role-based-access}{%
\section{4.3 Shared Identity and Authorisation
Backbone}\label{centralised-authorisation-and-role-based-access}}

To support the role-based access control and authentication needs of the platform, a centralised model needs to
be used for identity resolution. The sections below explain high-level details on the implementation that
the project used for this.

\hypertarget{jwt-verification-and-role-guards}{%
\subsection{4.3.1 JWT usage}\label{jwt-verification-and-role-guards}}

The platform uses a single JWT access-token model as the common
authentication contract across both user facing clients and backend services.
After sign in, the User Service issues the JWT token to clients, who store it and attach it
as bearer tokens in the authorisation header for all subsequent requests.
Backend services then verify the token on each request to confirm the identity and role of the caller.
As JWT tokens are self-contained and portable, they can be easily passed along with requests across different services without needing to query a central session store.


\hypertarget{centralised-identity-resolution}{%
\subsection{4.3.2 Centralised Identity
Resolution}\label{centralised-identity-resolution}}

Beyond the authentication workflows, User Service also serves as a
centralised and authoritative unit for identity resolution. Downstream
services first rely on User Service to confirm who the caller is and
what role they hold, then layer their own domain-specific checks on top
of that identity. This keeps role and ownership logic consistent across
quiz, class, AI, and game features.

\hypertarget{design-tradeoffs-and-limits}{%
\section{4.4 Design Tradeoffs and
Limits}\label{design-tradeoffs-and-limits}}

\hypertarget{rationale-for-an-in-house-user-service}{%
\subsection{4.4.1 Rationale for an In-House User
Service}\label{rationale-for-an-in-house-user-service}}

An early decision was made to develop an identity management service internally from
scratch instead of using an existing library or provider. The
platform needed two account types with noticeably different lifecycles,
teacher ownership of student access, and downstream identity checks that
are tightly tied to the rest of the platform. Adopting an existing
library would likely have required significant code adaptation and workarounds
to fit the unique needs of the platform, which would defeat the
purpose of using a pre-built solution in the first place.

Building an in-house user service allowed for a more seamless integration
with the rest of the platform, and it also provided more flexibility to
design the account models and authentication flows in a way that best
supports the key user journeys of the platform.

\hypertarget{centralisation-tradeoffs}{%
\subsection{4.4.2 Centralisation
Tradeoffs}\label{centralisation-tradeoffs}}

The choice to centralise authentication and identity resolution in one
place improves consistency, but it also creates a critical dependency.
If User Service is unavailable, many identity or role scoped operations across the
platform will also stall. Even so, centralisation was still regarded as the better
tradeoff here. Duplicating sensitive authorisation logic across several
services would have raised regression risk, and make the system a lot
harder to maintain in the long run, as any change to the identity model would
require updates across multiple services instead of just one.

\hypertarget{quiz-formats}{%
\section{5.1 Teacher Journey: Quiz Management}\label{quiz-formats}}

For teachers, a key user journey is the management of quizzes, which includes
the following steps:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Create the a quiz for a topic and learning objective.
  \item Assign created quizzes to students
  \item Review student attempts and performance on the assigned quiz.
  \item Update existing quizzes based on the review of student performance.
  \item Remove quizzes that are no longer relevant or needed.
\end{enumerate}


\hypertarget{iterative-authoring-and-versioning}{%
\subsection{5.1.1 Iterative Authoring and Versioning}\label{iterative-authoring-and-versioning}}

Teachers may want to update quiz content over time, either to fix errors,
improve clarity, or adjust the difficulty based on student performance.
However, the previous version of the quiz might already have been
attempted by students, and changing the quiz content directly would lead
to inconsistent schedule specific analytics and an inconsistent experience for students who have already attempted the quiz,


To prevent inconsistencies, one of the key design decisions was to make
quiz content immutable after creation.
Instead of direct changes, a versioning system is used to handle updates, allowing teachers to update quizzes
without affecting historical attempts unless they explicitly choose to.

Scheduled quizzes can then be linked to specific versions of a quiz,
and student attempts on that schedule are tied to that quiz version. Students will
therefore all receive the same consistent quiz experience even as teachers
update their quiz content, and past attempts can still be reviewed
without confusion even after content updates.

\hypertarget{basic-quiz}{%
\subsection{5.1.2 Quiz Type Variety}\label{basic-quiz}}

Different topics and learning outcomes require different forms of
retrieval practice. As such, the quiz service has been implemented with
support for multiple quiz formats, each with different interaction
patterns and content structures.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Basic Quiz}: a flexible format that supports a range of question types and structures, suitable for most use cases. Includes support for
  multiple-choice, multiple response, short answer, lists, and has a multi-part question structure.
  \item \textbf{Rapid Quiz}: a faster-paced format with a simpler interaction pattern of timed multiple-choice questions, designed for fluency practice.
  \item \textbf{Crossword Quiz}: a format that presents questions in a crossword puzzle format, adding a game-like interaction while still being retrieval-focused.
  \item \textbf{True/False Quiz}: a format that only supports true/false questions, designed for quick fact-checking and reinforcement of key concepts.
\end{enumerate}

Additional quiz types were also implemented to reduce repetitive teacher
work in cases where the question structure is predictable. These include

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Rapid Arithmetic Quiz}: a format that automatically generates arithmetic questions based on
  selected operations and ranges, designed for quick practice of basic math skills.
  \item \textbf{Crossword Bank Quiz}: a format that allows teachers to provide a bank of terms and clues,
  and generates multiple distinct crossword instances from that bank, allowing for efficient creation of many crossword quizzes.
\end{enumerate}

More detailed implementation for each quiz type can be found in
Developer Guide, specifically Sections 4.7.4, 5.4, and 5.5.

\hypertarget{generating-of-crossword-puzzles}{%
\subsection{5.1.3 Crossword Generation Algorithm}\label{generating-of-crossword-puzzles}}

Crossword quizzes are a unique format that requires an additional step which is
the generation of a grid based on the provided words. Having teachers to
manually create crossword grids would be too time consuming and technically
challenging, which would severely hinder the usability of the crossword format and goes
against the goal of making the platform as user-friendly and efficient for teachers as possible.

As such, the platform includes an implementation of crossword generation that can generate
crossword grids based on a list of words and clues provided by teachers.

The crossword generation method used is self-developed as no suitable library
or framework was found for this specific task.

Here is the high level pseudocode for the crossword generation algorithm:
\vspace{0.5cm}

\begin{center}
\begin{minipage}{0.95\linewidth}
\begin{spacing}{1.5}
\noindent\rule{\linewidth}{0.4pt}
\noindent Algorithm 5.1: Crossword generation\par\vspace{-0.35\baselineskip}
\noindent\rule{\linewidth}{0.4pt}
\begingroup
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Input:} list of words and clues.
  \item Sort the words by descending length.
  \item Seed the grid with the longest word at the centre.
  \item For each remaining word:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item Find all legal placements that overlap existing words.
    \item Rank the placements by overlap potential and compactness.
    \item If a legal placement exists, place the word in the best ranked position.
    \item If no placement is made in a full pass, start a new island and continue.
  \end{enumerate}
  \item Crop the grid to the minimal bounding box.
  \item \textbf{Output:} crossword grid.
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{-0.4\baselineskip}
\noindent\rule{\linewidth}{0.4pt}
\endgroup
\end{spacing}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

The greedy logic used here is to find a solution that optimises for compactness and overlap,
which are two key factors that contribute to the quality of a crossword grid.
The algorithm is not guaranteed to find the optimal solution, but it is efficient and produces good results
in practice for typical crossword sizes used in the platform. Lower-level generation logic and
implementation details can be found in Developer Guide Section 3.2.5.

This interface design is simple and focused, and it was intentionally made this

of the platform

\hypertarget{backend-managed-attempt-lifecycle}{%
\subsection{5.2.4 Infrastructure to Support a Smooth Student

A key consideration when designing the student experience was that it was phone-based.
Mobile usage is naturally prone to interruptions, as students may switch apps, lose connectivity, or
leave and return later. As such, the system needs to be designed to support a smooth experience even with
connectivity and usage interruptions.

Quiz attempt management is therefore handled entirely in the
backend, with the phone interface serving mainly as just an interaction surface
for students to submit answers and receive feedback.

Attempt validation, grading, timers are all enforced in the backend, and the client application simply
reflects the current state of the attempt as stored in the backend. This means that even if a student
gets interrupted during an attempt, they can simply resume it later without losing their progress.
For time-limited quizzes, the timers are also enforced in the backend through a Redis-based timing mechanism,
which means that even if a student changes their device's clock, or exits and re-enters the quiz,
the backend will still enforce the correct remaining time for the attempt.

\hypertarget{randomised-quizzes}{%
\section{5.3 Teacher Journey: Assigning Randomised Quizzes Fairly and
Consistently}\label{randomised-quizzes}}

For certain quiz types, such as the Rapid Arithmetic quiz and the
Crossword Bank quiz, the system has to generate randomised content from
the configuration provided by teachers. This means that multiple unique
quizzes could be generated from the same quiz, which could lead to
inconsistencies in the student experience if not handled carefully.

To ensure a consistent experience, the quiz system
generates quiz content when scheduled, rather than at
attempt time. When a teacher schedules a quiz that requires
randomisation, the specific quiz content for that
schedule is generated and stored as part of the scheduled quiz record. All
students who attempt that particular scheduled quiz then receives the same
generated content.

This approach is justified by two key usage concerns:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Fairness: Students in the same assignment context should not receive
  arbitrarily different quiz content, especially when quiz scores feed
  into overall progress and competitive leaderboards.
\item
  Analytics consistency: Teachers are shown per-question breakdowns in
  the dashboard, and those breakdowns are much harder to interpret if
  students receive different underlying questions.
\end{enumerate}

As such, assignment of randomised quizzes is ``schedule-anchored''. For
any particular scheduled quiz, generated quiz content is fixed and
shared across all students who attempt that scheduled quiz.

\hypertarget{extensibility-and-modularity-of-the-quiz-system}{%
\section{5.4 Maintainability Consideration: Extending Quiz Types}\label{extensibility-and-modularity-of-the-quiz-system}}

Because retrieval practice can take many forms, the quiz system is
purposefully designed to grow without requiring major refactors each
time a new quiz format is introduced. This improves maintainability and
allows the platform to evolve based on user feedback and changing
educational needs.

This is achieved through a registry-based system. A base quiz
schema defines key quiz fields and logic such as versioning, scheduling,
attempt management and event propagation,
and then specific quiz types extends from the base schema.

Each supported quiz type defines its own logic in its own file,
and all defined quiz types are registered into the registry on system startup,
extending from the base quiz schema. The registry is then used to route
quiz-related operations such as quiz creation, retrieval, and grading to
the correct quiz type logic based on the quiz type specified in the request.

The quiz types also define structures for quiz-specific analytic breakdowns
and AI generation prompts, which allow new quiz types to be easily integrated
into analytics and AI generation features without needing to change the logic
in Class and AI services.

Overall, this structure keeps the teacher journey of quiz management stable over
time. Even if new types of quizzes are added, the core management experience
remains consistent as new quiz types extend from the same base schema and contract,
and therefore integrate seamlessly into existing workflows of creation, scheduling, attempt,
analytics, and AI generation.

\hypertarget{schedule-crud-flow}{%
\subsection{6.2.1 Supporting Structure: Scheduling Logic}\label{schedule-crud-flow}}

The teacher journey for scheduling is simple from the user perspective, as the teacher
simply needs to assign quizzes on a single scheduling interface. But to support
a consistent and reliable scheduling experience, the system needs to keep three connected
parts of the system in sync. These include:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Schedule metadata (Class Service): the schedule details such as which quiz is assigned to which class,
  when the schedule window is, how many attempts are allowed, and how the quiz contributes to the overall score.
  \item Quiz attempts (Quiz Service): the students' attempts on the scheduled quizzes, which need to be correctly
  linked to the schedule stored in the Class Service.
  \item Analytics reporting (Class Service): the teacher-facing analytics and reporting that are built from
  the attempt data, which also need to be correctly linked to the schedule and updated when schedules are edited.
\end{enumerate}

As such, scheduling is a cross-domain workflow that requires careful coordination between Class and Quiz Service.
To ensure consistency, an event driven approach is used to link the scheduling metadata, quiz attempts, and analytics reporting together.

The following are the key steps in the scheduling workflow:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item When a quiz is scheduled, the schedule entry is created in the Class
    Service with the relevant quiz identifiers, the schedule window, along
    with other policy details such as attempt limits and score contribution.
  \item When students try to attempt the quiz, Quiz Service checks the schedule
    details from Class Service to determine if the quiz is currently available for the student,
    and to enforce attempt limits.
  \item On attempt completion, the Quiz Service emits attempt events that include the schedule identifiers,
    which allows the Class Service to link the attempt outcomes back to the correct schedule for analytics compilation.
\end{enumerate}

Proper compensating events are also emitted when schedules and quizzes are edited or deleted.

If a scheduled quiz is edited or deleted, a schedule change event is emitted by the class service,
which is consumed by Quiz Service to invalidate attempts linked to that schedule as needed,
and then invalidation events are passed back to Class Service again to update the analytics projections.

If a quiz that is linked to an active schedule is deleted, a quiz deletion event is emitted by Quiz Service,
which is consumed by Class Service to delete the schedules making use of that quiz.

More specific details about the quiz/class linking and event flows can be found within Developer Guide Section 3.2.

\hypertarget{analytics-and-projection-models}{%
\section{6.3 Supporting Structure: Analytics using Projection
Models}\label{analytics-and-projection-models}}

After the students have worked through scheduled quizzes, the platform should
provide the teacher with a tool for analysing the class performance. It will allow the
teacher to identify weak topics or students, and adjust/iterate on the quizzes to better
target weak areas in the next round of scheduling.

To support this, the platform needs to maintain analytics across different dimensions such as
student-based performance and schedule-based performance, These analytics need to be updated reasonably
quickly, be accurate and consistent, while being able to scale as the number of students and attempts grow.
To enable analytics without excessive system load, the analytics system is designed with projection models that are
asynchronously updated in the background based on attempt events emitted by Quiz Service. At request time,
the analytics views simply query the latest projections to provide insights to teachers, so no
heavy computations need to be done on the fly.

For updating projections, class service consumes the attempt events emitted by quiz service, and calculates the
appropriate deltas that should be applied to existing projections. Current analytics include student topic performance,
subject performance and overall performance and participation. Where multiple attempts are allowed for a schedule,
the analytics are based on a single ``canonical'' attempt per student and schedule, which is currently defined as the
best valid attempt.

Attempt invalidation events are also handled in this pipeline. If
attempts are edited or invalidated due to schedule or quiz changes,
projection updates apply reverse aggregation so outdated results are
removed from both all projections.

\hypertarget{chapter-7-ai-assisted-quiz-generation}{%
\chapter{Chapter 7 --- AI-Assisted Quiz
Generation}\label{chapter-7-ai-assisted-quiz-generation}}

One of the platform's main value propositions is its ability to support
retrieval practice at scale. To make frequent quiz-based practice
feasible in real classroom settings, the AI generation feature is
designed to reduce the time and effort required for teachers to produce
large volumes of quiz content.

Section 2.3 of the literature review identified that LLMs can
substantially reduce authoring time, but may also face risks such as
drift from syllabus, ambiguity and factual errors.

As such, the implementation decisions here follow a teacher-in-the-loop
workflow in which AI assists with initial drafting, and the web
interface then provides an intuitive interface for teachers to review
and edit the generated content. To further mitigate the potential risks
of syllabus drift and factual errors, the design of the generation
workflow will use structured prompting, with generation constraints and
narrow generation tasks for each request.

\hypertarget{user-workflow-and-interface-design}{%

The main teacher journey that this service should enable is a guided
system that allows a teacher to configure a request that generates
useful quiz outputs for classroom use. The following are the key
steps in the teacher journey for AI-assisted quiz generation:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Open the AI generation wizard in the teacher dashboard.
  \item Configure generation settings and optionally upload context documents.
  \item Submit the generation job and monitor progress.
  \item Return to review and edit generated drafts once processing completes.
\end{enumerate}

To support this journey, a dedicated generation interface is implemented in
the teacher dashboard. The interface provides clear options for the
teacher to select from, along with free-text inputs for instructions, and

takes the configured options and documents, and runs a structured generation

\hypertarget{typed-document-parsing-and-context-construction}{%
\section{7.2 Supporting Structure: Typed Document Grounding for
Generation}\label{typed-document-parsing-and-context-construction}}

To properly make use of the reference documents uploaded by teachers,
a typed document parsing and context construction system is used to
process the uploaded documents into context chunks that are fed into
the generation prompts of individual quizzes.

Uploaded documents must be classified by the teacher as
``syllabus'', ``question-bank'', ``subject-content'', or
``other'' when they are uploaded. Each document type is then
handled in a different way during context construction.

The following are the specific processing strategies for each document type:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  ``syllabus'': syllabus text is joined together and truncated, and
  then used as a ``constraint'' block which is inserted in full as
  context for generation in every single quiz. This means that context
  extracted from syllabus documents will be present in the prompt for
  every quiz. This is consistent with prior educational retrieval work
  suggesting that syllabus documents serve as an important grounding
  source, since they contain critical logistical
  constraints and factual information that models need access during
  generation. (Fernandez et al., 2024)
\item
  ``question-bank'': text extracted from question-bank documents is
  split into smaller units, and the units of text are rotated and used
  as context across the requested quizzes. This means that for large
  question bank documents uploaded, quizzes in the same batch will
  receive different subsets of the question bank as context, allowing for
  a wider variety of generated quiz content.
\item
  ``subject-content'': text extracted from subject-content
  documents is also split into units, but is rotated across quizzes in
  slightly larger chunks compared to question-bank text. This is because
  subject content is generally more dense and informative, and it is
  more important to keep the integrity of the content when splitting it up.
  This is supported by work showing that more semantically coherent,
  context-preserving chunks are often preferable when source material
  contains connected explanations, because overly aggressive splitting
  can fragment context, reduce retrieval completeness
  and hurt generation quality. (Merola \& Singh, 2025; Wang et al., 2025).
\item
  ``other'': text extracted from documents classified as
  ``other'' is treated as supplementary context. It is chunked
  conservatively and appended as additional reference notes at the end
  of the prompt.
\end{itemize}

This typed document parsing and context construction system is
supported by retrieval-augmented generation literature,
which suggests that handling of documents should be adaptive based
on the role, structure, and semantic density, rather than using
a single uniform treatment. This approach allows for a clearer semantic role to each
piece of context, which improves the relevance of generated items. (Wang et al., 2025; Shaukat et al., 2026; Merola \& Singh,
2025).

A simple OCR pipeline is also used if documents are identified to not
contain extractable text, which allows teachers to upload image-based
documents such as scanned worksheets and still have the content extracted for
generation.

\hypertarget{generation-pipeline-and-prompting-strategies}{%
\section{7.3 Supporting Structure: Two-Pass Generation Pipeline and
Prompting}\label{generation-pipeline-and-prompting-strategies}}

To create a more consistent and reliable generation experience,
a two-pass generation pipeline is used, with structured prompting
for both a planning and a generation pass.
This two-pass structure is supported by prior work showing that complex
generation tasks can benefit from being decomposed into a planning stage
followed by an execution stage, rather than attempting to solve the
entire task in a single pass (Webb et al., 2023).


The planning prompt includes the following items structured in a neat
format.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  The teacher's instructions
\item
  Up to 10 context blocks parsed from documents (as described in section
  7.2)
\item
  The number of quizzes and their types
\end{enumerate}

The LLM is then asked to return a strict JSON blueprint for the quizzes in
the entire batch.

In the next step, the blueprint is used to construct individual prompts for each quiz,
which includes the following items:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  The blueprint for that particular quiz
\item
  Context block processed from uploaded documents
\item
  The prompts and structure specific to the quiz type being generated
  (e.g.~basic, rapid, crossword), retrieved from quiz service
\item
  Constraints based on selected generation settings
  (e.g.~number of questions to be included)
\end{enumerate}

Requests for quiz generation are sent to the LLM in parallel up to a configurable parallel
limit. A progress entry is created for each generation request which is reflected on the
frontend interface for monitoring.

To support this review flow, The raw model response for each generated quiz
is normalised by AI Service into a draft quiz format suitable for displaying
in the frontend interface. These drafts are then returned to the teacher in
the frontend where edits can be made.

The generated quizzes stay in a
draft state until the teacher chooses either to approve the quizzes or
to discard them altogether. Approved quizzes are then saved into the
teacher's quiz library where they can then be scheduled for students to
attempt.

This structure fulfils the teacher-in-the-loop workflow,
as all generated content stays as draft within the AI Service until
the teacher explicitly approves it, after which the content is saved into
their quiz library. Teachers maintain full control over the generated
content that they wish to use or discard.

\hypertarget{design-choices-and-tradeoffs}{%
\section{7.5 Design Choices and Tradeoffs}\label{design-choices-and-tradeoffs}}

The design choices in this section reflect tradeoffs made to balance quiz quality,
reliability, cost, and system complexity.

\subsection{7.5.1 Parallel Quiz Generation and Planning}

The system generates quizzes in parallel to save time and to improve reliability,
as shorter, individual requests are generally easier to retry and debug than one
large request for the whole batch. This aligns with prompt-engineering guidance
that recommends breaking complex tasks into smaller subtasks to improve consistency
and simplify troubleshooting (Anthropic, n.d.; Amazon Web Services, 2024).
However, parallel generation also means that the LLM receives less context in
each individual quiz generation request, leading to greater overlap in quizzes
within the same batch. This reflects a trade-off, since larger prompts may contain
more shared context, but language models do not always use long contexts robustly
(Liu et al., 2023).

The additional planning pass also mitigates the shared context problem, as a blueprint is first created for the whole
batch, which can then be used to guide the parallel generation of individual quizzes
with clearer differentiation. However, the additional pass incurs
additional cost and also introduces a critical point of failure
in the generation workflow. This was accepted as
a valid tradeoff to achieve better overall quality of generated quizzes, but it remains
a key limitation of the current generation design.

\subsection{7.5.2 Multimodal Content Limitations}

There are also limitations in extracting and producing multi-modal
content. The current document-processing pipeline is designed mainly for
extracting text. Images contained within uploaded documents are not
parsed as visual content, and generated quiz questions do not support
image generation or retrieving images from external sources.

This was an intentional design choice tradeoff, as reliable multi-modal support
would require additional vision-related components such as computer
vision models. These would significantly increase system complexity,
incur cost, latency and make prompt
construction and output validation a lot more complicated. As such, to
make the generated quizzes more reliable at scale, the generation scope
has been deliberately limited to text-based quizzes only.


\hypertarget{chapter-8-gamification-and-game-service-design}{%
\chapter{Chapter 8 --- Gamification and Game Service
Design}\label{chapter-8-gamification-and-game-service-design}}

\hypertarget{design-goals-of-the-gamification-subsystem}{%
\section{8.1 Key Goals of the Gamification
Subsystem}\label{design-goals-of-the-gamification-subsystem}}

Following the findings in the literature review (Chapter 2.2), the key
design goals of this gamification subsystem are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  Gamification should be tied to the main learning activity of the
  application rather than have it be a competing objective. The
  gamification system should offer rewards and progression together with
  progression in retrieval practice, and it should not override or
  affect the main academic activity in any other way.
\item
  Gamification should have a concrete progression system to encourage
  students to return. The literature review points out that points,
  rewards, leaderboards are clear signs of progression that should be
  integrated into the gamification system, and any other reward logic
  that encourages meaningless activities should be completely avoided.
\item
  Gamification should support self-expression. Avatar identification and
  customisation have been found to improve intrinsic motivation towards
  tasks. As such, a well-designed avatar system could be further tied
  into the learning activity by linking the unlocking of avatar
  customisation items to progress in the learning activity.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, the gamification features should generally be supportive
elements that improve the learning experience rather than add a
competing objective.

\hypertarget{design-for-supportive-gamification}{%
\section{8.2 Student Journey: Post-Attempt Feedback
Flow}\label{design-for-supportive-gamification}}

The most important feedback in this platform is still the academic
feedback that students receive after each quiz attempt, which was
discussed in detail in Chapter 5. To ensure that the gamification
features do not compete with the academic feedback, the design of the
post-attempt feedback flow is carefully structured.

The post-attempt journey for gamification includes the following steps:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The student submits their attempt.
  \item The gamification layer evaluates the outcome of that attempt and updates the student's progression state.
  \item The student is first shown the grade achieved for the attempt.
  \item They are then shown any score progression and changes in leaderboard position linked to completing the attempt.
  \item Finally, they are shown any rewards unlocked from that attempt, such as avatar items or badges.
\end{enumerate}

\hypertarget{leaderboards-streaks-and-overall-score}{%
\section{8.3 Student Journey: Leaderboards and Streaks}\label{leaderboards-streaks-and-overall-score}}

To support an engaging gamification experience, the gamification system
is designed to reward students for their academic performance and
consistency. This is done with two main segments, a progression system
that tracks and rewards students' overall performance, and a consistency
system that tracks and rewards students' regular engagement with the platform.

Leaderboards and streaks fall under the consistency system, as they are designed to encourage consistent
performance and regular engagement with the platform. The student journey for this flow can be summarised as:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Student completes quizzes regularly and accumulates scores awarded by attempts.
  \item Student climbs up the leaderboard in rank and increases their daily attempt streak.
  \item Student receives rewards for reaching certain score milestones and streak milestones.
  \item Student returns daily for more attempts to maintain streaks and ranking.
\end{enumerate}

The leaderboards rank students based on their score, which is
accumulated from their performance in quiz attempts. There are three types of
leaderboards, an overall one, a monthly one and a weekly one. The separation of
leaderboards also allows for different time horizons of competition. This can be
motivating in the short term for weaker students as they still have a chance to
climb higher on the weekly and monthly leaderboards, even if they are far behind
on the overall leaderboard.

Streaks are also implemented to encourage consistency. A streak is
defined as the number of consecutive days on which a student completes
at least one quiz attempt. This provides students with a clear and
simple measure of their regular engagement with the platform. Streaks
can serve as a strong motivational cue, as missing a single day will
break the streak and reset the count. It therefore encourages students
to make quiz attempts a part of their daily routine, and it also
provides a sense of accomplishment for maintaining a high streak count.

The implementation of streaks and leaderboards are rather straightforward. Streaks are simply
as part of an attendance ledger stored in the student's game profile in Game Service.
The system stores the date of the student's last attempt, and the current streak count.
When a new attempt event is received or when a request is made to retrieve the streak count,
the system checks the date of the last attempt to determine whether to increment the streak count or reset it.
Since this logic only depends on a small amount of state within each student's profile, both streak updates
and streak retrieval can be performed in constant time. This makes the streak system computationally efficient,
while also keeping the logic simple and easy to maintain.

For leaderboards, the system simply stores a record of student overall, monthly and
weekly scores in the game profile, and when a request is made to retrieve the leaderboard,
the system retrieves the relevant scores for all students and sorts them to produce the leaderboard rankings.

On the progression side of the gamification system, two rule based reward types are implemented based on
the findings in the literature review: avatar customisation and displayable badges.

Both avatar items and badges are rewarded to students based on their performance in the platform, which
includes reaching certain score milestones, maintaining high streaks and achieving high ranks on the leaderboards.

These rewards serve as a more tangible way to complement the base
consistency based gamification features of the leaderboard ranking and
streaks. They provide students with specific goals to work towards and therefore encourage
a progression loop in which students are motivated to continue engaging with the platform in
order to unlock more rewards and further customise their avatar and profile.

The user journey for this reward workflow are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Student receives progression updates after attempting quizzes.
  \item Student unlocks rewards (avatar items and badges) based on milestone conditions.
  \item Student views and applies customisation items and equips earned badges in the profiles.
\end{enumerate}

\hypertarget{supporting-infrastructure-for-avatar}{%
\subsection{8.4.1 Supporting Structure for Avatar and Badge
Rewards}\label{supporting-infrastructure-for-avatar-and-badge-rewards}}

To support the features listed above, a set of rules is defined in the Game Service that
specifies the conditions under which students can unlock avatar items and earn badges.

Avatar cosmetics are rewarded at certain point thresholds to provide a
clear and consistent reward structure. For example, students might
unlock a new avatar item for every 100 points they earn.

Badges are awarded for specific achievements, such as maintaining a 30-day streak,
being in the top 3 of the weekly/monthly leaderboard at the end of the
period, or reaching a score milestone such as 1000 points.

The specific thresholds and conditions for rewards can be adjusted by the teacher at
a class level, allowing for some customisation based on the preferences
and motivations of each class.

At an implementation level, Game Service executes those rules by
consuming attempt events emitted by Quiz Service. Scores and other
relevant metrics are evaluated by Game Service against the defined reward rules,
and if the system determines that a reward should be granted, the student's
game profile is updated to reflect the new avatar items and badges.

To ensure the best execution of the avatar system for self-expression,
it was also designed to support a large variety of customisation options.
Items include options such as hats, glasses, shirts, outerwear, shoes, accessories and even pets.
As students are rewarded with more items, they can mix and match
different items to create more unique looks for their avatar.

\hypertarget{extensibility-of-assets}{%
\subsection{8.4.2 Extensibility of Assets}\label{extensibility-of-assets}}

The asset system (used for avatar cosmetics and badges) is designed with
extensibility in mind. New items can be added to the system without
needing to change the underlying rendering logic or data structures.
This allows for the gamification system to evolve and expand over time,
with new rewards and customisation options being added as needed without
requiring significant development effort.

For avatar cosmetics, the assets are organised into a fixed set of
slots (base avatar, eyes, mouth, upperwear, lowerwear, hair, outerwear,
shoes, accessories, pets). The assets are stored in a directory
structure that reflects these slots with a manifest file, and Game Service scans the asset
folders to build the item catalogue. This means that adding a new cosmetic item
only requires placing the asset file in the correct slot
folder and updating the manifest with the relevant metadata.

Every cosmetic asset in the system is designed with a shared 800x800
blueprint in mind. The artwork for each item is drawn in the correct
position within this shared canvas, which means that the renderer can
simply layer the equipped items on top of one another within the same
800x800 canvas without needing any additional positioning logic for each
item. As long as new items follow the blueprint, they can be added to
the system and rendered correctly without changes to rendering code.

\hypertarget{current-limitations-1}{%
\section{8.5 Current Limitations}\label{current-limitations-1}}

The current gamification system has some limitations. The reward
structure is currently quite simple, with only two types of rewards
(avatar items and badges) and a limited set of conditions for earning
those rewards. There is also no social or collaborative element to the
gamification system, such as team-based competitions. These features
could be explored in future iterations of the platform to further
enhance the motivational impact of the gamification system.

There is also currently a limited variety of avatar customisation
options, and the visual design of both the avatars and badges remains
fairly basic. However, expanding the range and quality of these assets
is mainly an art and asset-production effort, and was beyond the scope
of this project. The current implementation should be seen as a proof of
concept that demonstrates the technical viability of avatar
personalisation as a motivational feature, with plenty of space for
future expansion through improved artwork and a larger asset catalogue.

The more detailed topic catalogue and end-to-end workflow diagrams ca be found
in the separate Developer Guide, specifically Sections 2.1 to
2.5 and Sections 3.2.8 to 3.2.9.

\item \textbf{Variety}: whether the generated quizzes showed enough variety across the 5 generated quizzes per testcase
\end{itemize}

\hypertarget{evaluation-boundaries}{%
\section{9.4 Evaluation Boundaries}\label{evaluation-boundaries}}

The results across all three tests should be interpreted with caution. Both
user-testing phases were exploratory and limited in scale and scope
(11 usable responses in the initial phase, and 9 usable responses in the
follow-up phase). The surveys mainly focused on the usability of key
workflows and general attitudes towards the perceived value of the
platform and its features. While this feedback was useful for
identifying pain points and guiding development, it does not serve as a
definitive assessment of platform value. More rigorous evaluation would
still be needed, such as larger-scale studies and classroom deployment
to gather real-world usage data from teachers and students.

The AI generation feature also still has clear limitations in output
quality. Prompting methods remain relatively basic, and adoption of more
advanced prompting techniques or agentic approaches was limited at this
stage by resource constraints (cost and computation power). The
automated AI evaluation was also limited in scope, focusing mainly on
structural validity, variety, and reliability metrics, which do not
fully capture educational relevance and appropriateness of generated
content.

To fully assess the quality of AI-generated content, a more
comprehensive human evaluation is still needed. From the deterministic
evaluation pipeline in Chapter 9.3, generated quiz samples already exist and can be
used directly for SME review. Appendix F also includes a planned human
evaluation framework for this next phase. The framework uses a 1--5
rubric across four dimensions: alignment to prompt, alignment to
syllabus/curriculum intent, question correctness, and question variety.
Ratings are converted into a weighted manual score (with correctness
weighted highest), and can then be combined with the deterministic score for
a more complete overall assessment during future iterations.

\hypertarget{key-outcomes}{%
\section{10.1 Key Outcomes}\label{key-outcomes}}

This project set out to design and develop an education platform that
leverages AI to assist teachers in creating a reliable retrieval
practice tool for their students, with the goal of improving student
learning outcomes and reducing teacher workload.

The final prototype achieved a working end-to-end implementation of that
idea, with a teacher-facing web dashboard, a student-facing mobile
application, and backend services that support the core classroom
workflow. Additionally, the two rounds of user testing helped
to inform and refine key features to be more in line with user needs.
A evaluation of different LLMs for the AI generation feature was also
conducted to identify a good model candidate for the platform's specific use case.

In summary, the main outcomes of this project include:
\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  A full-stack quiz platform with working teacher and student clients,
  implemented using an event-based microservices architecture.
\item
  A quiz model that supports multiple quiz types, scheduling policies,
  versioning, and attempt tracking.
\item
  An AI-assisted quiz generation pipeline designed with
  teacher-in-the-loop control, where generated content can be reviewed
  and edited by teachers before being finalised.
\item
  A game service that supports student progress tracking with
  leaderboards, streaks, cosmetic rewards, badges, avatar rendering, and
  reward notifications.
\item
  A deterministic evaluation framework for testing AI-generated content
  for reliability, structural errors, and variety.
\item
  Two rounds of user testing with teachers to evaluate the usability of
  core workflows and the perceived value of the platform, which helped guide
  iterative improvements to the design and features.
\end{enumerate}

\hypertarget{future-work-roadmap}{%
\section{10.2 Future Work Roadmap}\label{future-work-roadmap}}

\hypertarget{workflow-refinement-and-adoption-readiness}{%
\subsection{10.2.1 Workflow Refinement and Adoption
Readiness}\label{workflow-refinement-and-adoption-readiness}}

An important next step would be to streamline the core workflows even
further.
It would be a good idea to include template quizzes that teachers can
use as a starting point, which they can then edit and customise to their
needs. This would make it easier for teachers to get started with the platform
and reduce onboarding effort.

An additional improvement would be the support of rich text and/or LaTeX
rendering in quizzes. This was a common pain point mentioned in the user
testing, especially for subjects like Mathematics and Science where the
ability to write in mathematical notation is crucial.

\hypertarget{further-evaluation-and-experimental-testing}{%
\subsection{10.2.2 Further Evaluation and Experimental
Testing}\label{further-evaluation-and-experimental-testing}}

Another important area of future work would be to conduct further
evaluation and experimental testing of the platform to gather more
conclusive evidence on its effectiveness and impact on student learning
outcomes.

A proper evaluation should be conducted in classrooms on whether the
platform's implementation of retrieval practice is actually effective in
improving learning outcomes. The current project uses existing
literature on retrieval practice to justify its design, but retrieval
practice can come in many forms, and this specific implementation of
retrieval practice through a phone-based application with basic quiz
features and a gamification layer for engagement is a specific
interpretation of retrieval practice. As such, it would be good to
conduct an experimental study to compare the learning outcomes of
students using this platform against a control group that does not use
the platform, or that uses a different form of retrieval practice.

More experimentation should also be done on the AI generation workflow.
The current AI workflow uses generic prompting techniques with two
passes of generation and basic document processing methods. Future
iterations could explore more advanced techniques such as
retrieval-augmented generation, or agentic approaches where multiple
models with different functionalities (such as a validator agent, a
planning agent) work together to generate higher-quality content.
Open-source models could also be fine-tuned with local educational
content to potentially enhance the relevance and quality of generated
content. With enough local syllabus and curriculum materials, there is
also potential for the development of a custom model that is
specifically trained on local educational content, or for large amounts
of content to be indexed into a vector database to support
retrieval-augmented generation. This would allow the model to reference
more specific materials that are relevant to the teacher's prompt, which
could further enhance the relevance and quality of the generated quizzes
for the Singapore context.

\hypertarget{final-remarks}{%
\section{10.3 Final Remarks}\label{final-remarks}}

This project was inspired by my own experiences as a student, as I found
typical revision materials such as notes and other platforms to be
insufficient and ineffective for me. I found that I was only able to
truly learn and retain information when I was actively recalling and
applying the knowledge. This project was an attempt to create a
tool that could easily support this kind of active recall practice, and
to leverage the potential of AI to ease the process of creating such
materials for students. Overall, this project has been a valuable
learning experience for me, as it required me to apply a wide range of
skills and knowledge I had picked up over my time in university.
Software design, system architecture, full-stack development, AI
prompting, and user testing were all important areas in improving the
quality of the final product, and I had to learn and apply new concepts
and techniques to successfully complete the
project.

I also learnt quite a lot about the educational landscape in Singapore, especially in
the context of primary education. And while this platform has already
implemented many of the core features that I had envisioned for the project,
there are still areas for improvement and further development.
Even if this platform does not achieve widespread adoption, I believe that it
can still serve as a useful prototype and proof of
concept for how retrieval testing and AI generation can be used to
support education in the future.
